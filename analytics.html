Advanced Analytics

Why teams use advanced analytics

Advanced stats are mostly about context and comparison. MLB teams have to compare players across different ballparks, different eras, and different roles. That’s why “adjusted” stats (like OPS+) and “all-in-one value” stats (like WAR) are common in front offices.

Scouts still matter a lot, but analytics helps teams make consistent decisions for drafting, trades, free agency, and roster construction by combining many data points into a clearer picture.

What is WAR?

WAR (Wins Above Replacement) estimates a player’s total value in wins compared to a replacement-level player. It rolls offense, defense, and baserunning (and for pitchers, run prevention) into one number.

Teams like WAR because it gives a quick “overall value” snapshot and allows easier comparisons across positions. The important thing to remember is that WAR is an estimate, not a perfect truth, and the exact value can differ depending on the model used.

OPS+

OPS+ adjusts a player’s OPS for park and league effects; 100 is league average, above 100 is better than average. Teams like it because raw hitting numbers can be misleading in hitter-friendly or pitcher-friendly parks.

In plain terms, OPS+ helps answer: “How good was this hitter compared to the league, once we adjust for context?”

Great historical examples (high level)

  • Overall value (WAR): Babe Ruth and Willie Mays are classic all-around benchmarks.
  • Modern all-around value: Mike Trout is often cited as a standout modern WAR example.
  • Adjusted hitting (OPS+): Babe Ruth, Ted Williams, and Barry Bonds are commonly cited as elite levels.

These examples are meant as recognizable references. Comparing across eras is tricky, which is exactly why adjusted stats exist in the first place.
